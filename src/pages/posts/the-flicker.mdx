<p>
<b>the flicker</b> (1966) is a film by <b>tony conrad</b>, best known for being one of the pioneers of musical minimalism and drone. among other things he was also interested in the psychedelic effect of stroboscopic lights, which he extensively studied and decided to play with in his first film.
</p>

<p>
<b>the flicker</b> consists of different patterns of black and white frames which when projected create a powerful strobe effect. the film gives the viewer access to a myriad of visual hallucinations, colors, rotating fractals, rothkos, exploding squares, holes and melting spirals, all hidden inside the quick pulse of these empty b/w frames; it's an experience genuinely comparable to the effect of a psychedelic drug.
</p>

<PostImage src={flickerVisualization} alt="Visualization of the Flicker pattern" />
<p>
not only does <b>the flicker</b> strip cinema down to its elementary parts - light and time - but it transcends the film form in itself. when this film is played, it does not exist just inside the screen; it <i>escapes</i> the screen, it plays itself on the surrounding walls, on the viewer's skin. it is watched and felt even with eyes closed. it creates images which exist directly inside the brain, images which will be different and unique each time.
</p>

<p>
i am completely fascinated by its experimental and transcendental quality. it's a radically unique approach to what was, at the time, the newest artform. <b>conrad</b> created something more than cinema, something maybe more aptly described as a film-object, or a film-machine. it is this train of thought that inspired me to reconstruct it digitally - a thought that <b>conrad</b> also shared, as it is known he recreated <b>the flicker</b> on an amiga computer, but never released to the public.
</p>

<p>
in order to recreate <b>the flicker</b>, i used an algorithm to mark each frame of the original cut as either white or black. the resulting data was used to rebuild the film using solid <b>#000000</b> and <b>#ffffff</b> colors, thus eliminating the imperfections of the film roll and mantaining the same rhythm and strobes of the original.
this reconstruction also removes the introductory segment and music in order to enhance its digital essentiality. the format is <b>1920x1080</b> and it plays at the original speed of <b>24</b> fps.
</p>
