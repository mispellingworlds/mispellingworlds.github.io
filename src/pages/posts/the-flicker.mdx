<p>
<b>the flicker</b> (1966) is a film by <b>tony conrad</b>, best known for being one of the pioneers of musical minimalism and drone. among his other interests, the psychedelic effect of stroboscopic lights, which he extensively studied and decided to explore with his first film.
</p>

<p>
<b>the flicker</b> consists in fact of different patterns of black and white frames which when projected create a powerful strobe effect. the film gives the viewer access to a myriad of visual hallucinations, colors, rotating fractals, explosions, holes, all hidden in the pulsation of these solid b/w frames; it's an experience genuinely comparable to the effect of a psychedelic drug.
</p>

<PostImage src={flickerVisualization} alt="Visualization of the Flicker pattern" />
<p>
not only does <b>the flicker</b> strip cinema down to its elementary elements - light and time - but it trascends the film form itself. when this film is played, it is not simply played on the screen, but it escapes it and plays itself on the surrounding walls, on the viewer's skin. it is watched and felt even with eyes closed, it creates images which exist directly inside the brain, images which will be different and unique each time.
</p>

<p>
i am completely fascinated by its trascendental quality. it's a radically unique approach to what at the time was the newest artform. <b>conrad</b> created something more than cinema, something maybe more aptly described as a film-object, a well designed machine. it is this train of thought that inspired me to reconstruct it digitally - a thought that <b>conrad</b> also shared, as it is known he recreated <b>the flicker</b> on an amiga computer, although it was never released to the public.
</p>

<p>
i used an algorithm to mark each frame of the original cut as either white or black. the resulting data was used to rebuild the film using solid <b>#000000</b> and <b>#ffffff</b> colors, thus eliminating the imperfections of the film roll.
this reconstruction also removes the introductory segment and music from the original, in order to enhance its digital purity. the format is <b>1920x1080</b> and it plays at the original speed of <b>24</b> fps.
</p>
